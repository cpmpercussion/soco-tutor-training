## About this guide

Companion notes to `lectures/03-feedback.md`. For the **presenter** (running order,
timings, activity setup) and **participants** (what to expect).

The module moves from *what assessment is for* → *what makes feedback effective* →
*evaluating real feedback* → *rubrics and marking*.

## At a glance

- **Slot:** ~90 minutes (day schedule: 13:30–15:00, before afternoon tea).
- **Room:** table groups; sticky notes; PollEverywhere for the adjectives list.
- **Discussion-led:** most content slides now open a short discussion — use them.

## Facilitation notes

- **Effective Feedback task:** the adjectives collected on PollEverywhere become a
  shared vocabulary — refer back to them during the principles slides.
- **Principles (3 themed slides):** the reframe up front is important — for new
  tutors who think feedback = harsh critique, stress that feedback is *information
  that helps a learner improve*.
- **Evaluating feedback (30 min):** three markers' feedback shown for the same
  work in Code / Essay / Maths. Ask: which is most effective, *why*, and what
  impact on the student? Pick the discipline(s) closest to your cohort if pressed for time.
- **Reading a Rubric (10 min):** in pairs, pick **one** of the six example rubrics
  (four generic incl. a programming rubric; two are ANU computing courses assessing
  *creative, music-themed* work). Identify the three essential features (criteria /
  quality definitions / scoring strategy) and two design choices (generic vs
  task-specific, analytic vs holistic, number of quality levels). Debrief straight
  into *Scoring strategies for code*.

## Handoff notes for a new presenter

- **Set up your own PollEverywhere** for the adjectives list.
- The **"Grade Level to Marks"** slide is commented out (kept low-level detail out
  of the room) — un-comment it if a marking-heavy cohort wants the mark-band tip.
- Key citations in the deck: Dawson (2017) on rubric design; Messer et al. (2025,
  ACM TOCE) on why a shared rubric alone doesn't guarantee consistent marking.

## Materials checklist

- Sticky notes + pens
- PollEverywhere poll (your own)
- Example rubrics visible on screen (optionally printed for pairs)

## Key messages

- Feedback is **information that helps a learner improve**, not a verdict — focus on the *work*, not the student.
- A **rubric** = evaluative criteria + quality definitions + a scoring strategy; being explicit about design choices helps a marking team stay consistent.
- **A rubric alone isn't enough** — assessor training and moderation matter (Messer et al., 2025).
